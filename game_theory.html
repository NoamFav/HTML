1. Fundamental Concepts

Game Theory: The mathematical study of strategic interaction between rational decision-makers (players) who must consider the actions and reactions of others when making decisions.

Core Components of a Game

  • Players: Decision-makers in the game
  • Strategies: Available actions for each player
  • Payoffs: Outcomes/utilities for each player given strategy combinations
  • Information: What each player knows when making decisions

Types of Games

Strategic Games (Normal Form): Players choose strategies simultaneously with no temporal structure and complete information about game structure.
Extensive Games (Sequential): Players move in a specific order with temporal structure and may involve incomplete information.
Cooperative vs Non-Cooperative: Cooperative games allow binding agreements; non-cooperative games require independent action.

Key Assumptions

Rationality: Players have well-defined preferences, seek to maximize payoffs, and can process information effectively.
Common Knowledge: All players know the game structure, the rationality of all players, and that everyone else knows these facts.

Solution Concepts

Dominant Strategy: A strategy that yields higher payoff regardless of opponents' actions.
  • Strictly Dominant: Always better than alternatives
  • Weakly Dominant: At least as good as alternatives, sometimes better
Iterated Elimination of Dominated Strategies: Process of removing dominated strategies sequentially to simplify games and find solutions.

Properties

  • Existence: Every finite game has at least one Nash equilibrium (in mixed strategies)
  • Uniqueness: Nash equilibria may not be unique
  • Efficiency: Nash equilibria may not be Pareto efficient

Finding Nash Equilibria

Best Response Method:
  1. For each player, identify best responses to opponent strategies
  2. Find intersections of best response correspondences
Cell-by-Cell Method (2x2 Games):
  1. For each cell, check if either player wants to deviate
  2. If no player wants to deviate, it's a Nash equilibrium

Refinements

  • Subgame Perfect Equilibrium: Nash equilibrium that remains Nash in every subgame
  • Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium: Combines sequential rationality with consistent beliefs
  • Trembling Hand Perfect: Equilibrium robust to small mistakes by opponents

4. Extensive Form Games

Components

Game Tree: Graphical representation showing:
  • Decision nodes: Points where players choose actions
  • Chance nodes: Points where random events occur
  • Terminal nodes: End points with payoffs
  • Branches: Available actions at each node
Information Sets: Collections of decision nodes where a player cannot distinguish between them.
  • Perfect Information: Each information set contains exactly one node
  • Imperfect Information: Some information sets contain multiple nodes

Backward Induction

Process:
  1. Start at terminal nodes
  2. Work backward through game tree
  3. At each decision node, choose action maximizing payoff
  4. Replace node with expected payoff
Sequential Prisoner's Dilemma: Player 1 chooses first (Cooperate/Defect), Player 2 observes and responds.
Analysis: Player 2 will always defect, so Player 1 should defect.
Centipede Game: Players alternately choose to continue or stop. Payoffs increase as game continues.
Theoretical Prediction: Stop immediately (backward induction)
Experimental Observation: Often continue for several rounds

7. Auction Theory

Auction Types

English Auction (Open Ascending): Price starts low and increases. Bidders drop out when price exceeds value. Winner pays final price.
Dutch Auction (Open Descending): Price starts high and decreases. First bidder to accept wins and pays accepted price.
First-Price Sealed-Bid: Simultaneous sealed bids. Highest bidder wins and pays their bid.
Second-Price Sealed-Bid (Vickrey): Simultaneous sealed bids. Highest bidder wins but pays second-highest bid.

Key Results

Revenue Equivalence Theorem: Under certain conditions, all standard auction formats yield the same expected revenue.
Vickrey Auction Properties:
  • Truthful bidding is dominant strategy
  • Efficient allocation (highest value wins)
  • Individual rationality satisfied

Mechanism Design Principles

  • Incentive Compatibility: Truth-telling is optimal strategy
  • Individual Rationality: Participation yields non-negative payoff
  • Revelation Principle: Any implementable social choice function can be implemented by truthful mechanism

8. Mechanism Design

Framework

Problem: Design game rules to achieve desired outcomes when players have private information.

Components:
  • Type space: Private information players might have
  • Mechanism: Rules specifying outcomes based on reported types
  • Implementation: Mechanism successfully achieves desired outcome

Impossibility Results

Gibbard-Satterthwaite Theorem: No voting mechanism with 3+ alternatives can be simultaneously non-dictatorial, strategy-proof, and onto.
Arrow's Impossibility Theorem: No social choice function can satisfy all desirable properties simultaneously.

Applications

  • Auction Design: Maximize revenue while maintaining efficiency
  • Voting Systems: Aggregate preferences fairly
  • Contract Theory: Align incentives between principals and agents

9. Behavioral Game Theory

Deviations from Rationality

  • Bounded Rationality: Limited computational abilities and information processing
  • Social Preferences: Caring about others' payoffs (altruism, inequality aversion, reciprocity)
  • Learning: Players adapt strategies based on experience

Experimental Findings

Ultimatum Game:
Theoretical prediction: Accept any positive offer
Experimental result: Rejection of "unfair" offers (< 30%)
Public Goods Games:
Theoretical prediction: Zero contribution
Experimental result: Substantial initial contribution, declining over time
Beauty Contest Game:
Theoretical prediction: Choose 0
Experimental result: Gradual convergence toward 0 over rounds

Psychological Factors

  • Framing Effects: How options are presented affects choices
  • Loss Aversion: Losses weigh more heavily than equivalent gains
  • Fairness Concerns: Willingness to sacrifice material payoffs for fairness

10. Applications in AI and Data Science

Multi-Agent Systems

  • Coordination Problems: Ensuring agents work together effectively
  • Resource Allocation: Distributing computational resources or data fairly
  • Distributed Optimization: Agents optimize local objectives while considering global outcome

Machine Learning

  • Adversarial Learning: Two networks compete (GANs - Generative Adversarial Networks)
  • Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning: Agents learn in environments with other learning agents
  • Federated Learning: Incentivizing data sharing while preserving privacy

Algorithmic Game Theory

  • Internet Auctions: Design of online advertising auctions (Google AdWords)
  • Network Games: Behavior in social networks, information cascades
  • Computational Social Choice: Voting algorithms and preference aggregation

Strategic Classification

Problem: Individuals may game their features to receive better algorithmic decisions.
Solutions: Robust classification, mechanism design for learning.

Market Design

  • Matching Markets: Pairing students to schools, doctors to hospitals
  • Two-Sided Markets: Platforms connecting buyers and sellers (eBay, Airbnb)
  • Prediction Markets: Aggregating information through betting mechanisms

üìã Key Theorems and Results

Existence Theorems

  • Nash's Theorem: Every finite game has at least one Nash equilibrium
  • Fixed Point Theorems: Brouwer, Kakutani for equilibrium existence

Impossibility Results

  • Arrow's Impossibility Theorem: Perfect voting systems don't exist
  • Gibbard-Satterthwaite: Strategy-proof voting is limited

Efficiency Results

  • First Welfare Theorem: Competitive equilibria are Pareto efficient
  • Revenue Equivalence: Auction formats yield equal expected revenue

üéØ Problem-Solving Strategies

For Strategic Form Games

  1. Check for dominant strategies
  2. Eliminate dominated strategies iteratively
  3. Look for pure strategy Nash equilibria
  4. If none exist, find mixed strategy equilibria using indifference conditions

For Extensive Form Games

  1. Draw complete game tree
  2. Identify information sets
  3. Use backward induction for perfect information
  4. Check for subgame perfect equilibrium

For Cooperative Games

  1. Define characteristic function
  2. Check core existence using balancedness
  3. Calculate Shapley value using formula or marginal contributions
  4. Verify individual rationality and efficiency

üîç Practical Analysis Framework

When Analyzing Strategic Situations:
  1. Identify Players: Who are the decision-makers?
  2. Define Strategies: What actions can each player take?
  3. Determine Payoffs: What does each player want to maximize?
  4. Assess Information: What does each player know and when?
  5. Predict Outcomes: What equilibria are likely to emerge?
  6. Evaluate Efficiency: How good are these outcomes?
Study Tips:
  • Focus on understanding intuition before diving into mathematics
  • Always connect abstract theory back to practical applications
  • Practice with simple examples before tackling complex problems
  • Remember that game theory provides insights into strategic thinking in AI and data science

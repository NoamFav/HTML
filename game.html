Game Theory Concepts

A comprehensive guide to bankruptcy problems, cooperative games, and strategic interactions

Bankruptcy Problems

Exploring fair distribution methods when resources are insufficient to meet all claims

Contested Garment Principle (CGP)

A fundamental principle from the Talmud that addresses how to divide a contested asset when multiple parties have claims. This principle forms the foundation for understanding fair division in situations of scarcity and provides insights into proportional reasoning in conflict resolution.

Talmud Rule (TAL)

A sophisticated rule derived from Talmudic sources that combines both proportional and equal treatment principles. The rule operates differently depending on whether the estate is above or below half the total claims, implementing a coalitional procedure for fair distribution.

Axioms of Rules

Mathematical properties that bankruptcy rules should satisfy, including anonymity, claims boundedness, estate monotonicity, and composition. These axioms help evaluate and compare different distribution methods.

Constrained Equal Award (CEA)

A rule that distributes the estate by giving each claimant an equal amount, subject to the constraint that no one receives more than their claim. This approach prioritizes equality while respecting individual claim limits.

Constrained Equal Loss (CEL)

A complementary rule to CEA that equalizes losses rather than awards. Each claimant suffers the same loss, constrained so that no one's award becomes negative. This method focuses on equal sacrifice principles.

Proportional Rule (PROP)

The most intuitive bankruptcy rule that distributes the estate in proportion to claims. If the estate is E and total claims are C, each claimant with claim c_i receives (c_i/C) × E. This maintains the relative ranking of claims.

Hydraulic Rationing

A dynamic approach to rationing that models the distribution process as water flowing through containers of different heights, representing different claim levels. This provides an intuitive physical interpretation of fair division.

Bilateral Consistency

An important axiom requiring that when some claimants leave with their allocated shares, the remaining problem should be solved the same way. This ensures procedural stability and prevents manipulation through strategic departures.

Cooperative Games

Analyzing situations where players can form binding coalitions and share benefits

Game Definitions

A cooperative game consists of a set of players N and a characteristic function v that assigns a value to each possible coalition. The function v: 2^N → ℝ represents the total payoff that coalition S can achieve independently.

The Core

The set of allocations that no coalition can improve upon by deviating. An allocation x is in the core if the sum equals v(N) and for every coalition S, the sum of payoffs to S's members is at least v(S). The core represents stable solutions.

Core Axioms

Key properties including efficiency (full distribution of value), individual rationality (players get at least their standalone value), and coalitional rationality (no coalition has incentive to deviate).

Shapley Value

A unique solution concept that assigns to each player their expected marginal contribution across all possible orders of coalition formation. The Shapley value φ_i(v) considers all permutations and averages each player's contribution.

Shapley Axioms

Four axioms uniquely characterize the Shapley value: efficiency, symmetry, dummy, and additivity. These properties ensure fair and consistent allocation of cooperative gains.

Nucleolus

A solution concept that minimizes the maximum complaint (excess) of any coalition. The nucleolus lexicographically minimizes the vector of excesses arranged in decreasing order, providing a compromise solution when the core is empty.

Applications

Cooperative game theory applies to cost sharing (utilities, transportation), resource allocation (airport runway costs, joint ventures), voting power analysis, and market regulation problems.

Extensive Form Games

Sequential decision-making with perfect and imperfect information

Rationality & Common Knowledge

Players are assumed to be rational (maximizing expected utility) and this rationality is common knowledge - everyone knows everyone is rational, everyone knows that everyone knows, and so on infinitely.

Game Theory Puzzles

Classic puzzles like the Centipede Game, Chain Store Paradox, and Backward Induction Paradox illustrate the tension between theoretical predictions and observed behavior in sequential games.

Actions vs Strategies

Actions are choices available at specific decision nodes, while strategies are complete plans specifying what action to take at every possible information set. A strategy is a function from information sets to actions.

Nash Equilibria

A profile of strategies where no player can unilaterally deviate and improve their payoff. In extensive form games, we can have multiple equilibria that may rely on non-credible threats at unreached information sets.

Subgame Perfect Equilibria

A refinement of Nash equilibrium requiring that strategies constitute a Nash equilibrium in every subgame. This eliminates non-credible threats and ensures rational play throughout the entire game tree.

Backward Induction

The solution method for finding subgame perfect equilibria in games with perfect information. Start from the terminal nodes and work backward, determining optimal actions at each decision node assuming rational play thereafter.

Bimatrix Games

Strategic interactions between two players with simultaneous decision-making

Pure vs Mixed Actions

Pure strategies involve choosing a single action with certainty. Mixed strategies are probability distributions over pure strategies, allowing players to randomize their choices to keep opponents uncertain.

Dominated Actions

An action is strictly dominated if another action always yields higher payoffs regardless of opponent's choice. Weakly dominated actions never do better and sometimes do worse. Rational players never use dominated strategies.

Best Reply Correspondences

For each of opponent's strategies, the best reply is the set of strategies that maximize a player's expected payoff. The best reply correspondence BR_i(s_{-i}) maps opponent strategies to optimal responses.

2×2 Game Equilibria

Every 2×2 game has at least one Nash equilibrium (possibly in mixed strategies). Games may have pure strategy equilibria, mixed strategy equilibria, or both. Common examples include Prisoner's Dilemma and Coordination games.

2×3 and 3×2 Games

Asymmetric games where players have different numbers of strategies. Analysis involves checking for pure equilibria first, then computing mixed equilibria by making players indifferent between their available strategies.

Correlated Equilibria

A generalization of Nash equilibrium where players condition their actions on signals from a correlation device. Players have no incentive to deviate from recommended actions given the information provided by the device.
